 4  ''' B{Resource Management} 
 5   
 6      The resource API provides the means to create, discover, manage and use 
 7      resources, in particular compute and storage resources (L{resource.Compute}, 
 8      L{resource.Storage}).  A L{resource.Manager} class acts as resource 
 9      provider, providing stateful resource instances. 
10   
11      B{Example}:: 
12   
13        # describe the resource requirements 
14        cd = saga.resource.ComputeDescription() 
15        cd.slots = 16 
16   
17        # obtain a handle to a suitable resource, and wait until it is active 
18        rm = saga.resource.Manager(url) 
19        cr = rm.create_compute(cd) 
20        cr.wait(saga.resource.State.Active) 
21   
22        # submit a large job onto the now active resource 
23        jd = saga.job.Description() 
24        jd.executable = '/bin/my/executable' 
25        jd.number_of_processes = 16 
26   
27        js = saga.job.Service(cr) 
28        j  = js.create_job(jd) 
29        j.run() 
30        j.wait() 
31   
32        # once the job is finished, we do not need the compute resource anymore: 
33        cr.destroy() 
34   
35   
36      Note that in the example above, there is no indication on how the resource 
37      is provided -- that is fully up to the resource manager.  The resource could 
38      be a existing compute cluster of that size, a advanced reservation slice on 
39      an even larger cluster, a pilot job slice on a cluster, or a virtual cluster 
40      provided via IaaS.  That is also the reason why resource instances are 
41      stateful: for most of the possible backends listed above, resources are not 
42      necessarily provisioned instantly, and they do have a finite lifetime. 
43   
44      The resource package also manages storage resources, in a manner very 
45      similar to example 1.  It does not handle the co-location of compute and 
46      data resources -- that is left to either the backend, or to the application 
47      itself. 
48  '''
